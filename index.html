What is a Rights Offering?

A rights offering (also called a rights issue) is a corporate action where a company raises capital by giving existing shareholders the opportunity to purchase additional shares at a discounted price, typically proportional to their current holdings.

Why Do Companies Conduct Rights Offerings?

Companies pursue rights offerings for several strategic reasons:

  • Capital Raising: Quickly raise funds without traditional underwriting costs
  • Financial Restructuring: Reduce debt, improve balance sheets during distress
  • Growth Initiatives: Fund expansion, acquisitions, or infrastructure investments
  • Shareholder Protection: Offer existing shareholders first opportunity before dilution
  • Lower Cost: Generally less expensive than traditional public offerings

Key Characteristics

  • Pro-Rata Distribution: Rights are distributed based on current shareholdings
  • Subscription Ratio: Determines how many rights are needed to purchase one share (e.g., 5 rights = 1 share)
  • Subscription Price: Typically offered at a discount (15-25%) to current market price
  • Tradeable Rights: Rights can often be sold if shareholder chooses not to exercise
  • Time-Limited: Rights expire on a specific date and become worthless if not exercised

Key Concepts & Terminology

Basic Subscription

The right to purchase shares based on your proportional ownership. If you own 1,000 shares and the ratio is 1:1, you can purchase 1,000 additional shares at the subscription price.

Oversubscription

The ability to request additional shares beyond your basic subscription allocation. These are only granted if other shareholders don't fully exercise their basic subscription rights.

Subscription Ratio

Defines how many rights are required to purchase one share. A ratio of "5 rights for 1 share" means you need 5 rights to buy 1 new share of common stock.

Dilution

When new shares are issued, each existing share represents a smaller percentage of ownership. Rights offerings allow shareholders to maintain their proportional ownership by participating.

Pro-Rata Ownership

Your proportional stake in the company. If you own 2% before the offering and exercise your full basic subscription, you maintain 2% ownership after.

Subscription Price

The discounted price at which shareholders can purchase new shares during the rights offering period.

Subscription Ratios Explained

Rights offerings often require multiple rights to purchase one share. This is called the subscription ratio.

Subscription Ratio Meaning Example
1 for 1 1 right = 1 share Own 1,000 shares → Get 1,000 rights → Can buy 1,000 shares
5 for 1 5 rights = 1 share Own 1,000 shares → Get 1,000 rights → Can buy 200 shares
3 for 2 3 rights = 2 shares Own 900 shares → Get 900 rights → Can buy 600 shares
Important: Fractional rights are typically rounded up or down based on the prospectus terms. Always check the specific offering documents for fractional share treatment.

Case Studies: Two Different Rights Offerings

Both companies raised capital through rights offerings, but for fundamentally different strategic reasons. Understanding these motivations is crucial for corporate actions professionals.

Rent the Runway (RENT)

"Financial Distress & Balance Sheet Restructuring"

Announcement Date: August 21, 2025
Offering Size: $12.5 million
Subscription Price: $4.08 per share (20% discount to 30-day VWAP)
Strategic Context: Part of a broader growth recapitalization with APS, STORY3, and Nexus Capital

The Situation

Rent the Runway, the disruptor that created the fashion rental category, found itself in a precarious financial position by mid-2025. Despite being a pioneer in the sharing economy and changing how women access designer fashion, the company was struggling with an unsustainable debt load and needed immediate balance sheet repair.

CEO Jennifer Hyman acknowledged that while the company had "executed a significant and successful strengthening of the business over the past 18 months" and brought it "to nearly free cash flow breakeven in 2024," the existing capital structure was preventing sustainable growth. The company needed more than incremental improvements—it needed a comprehensive recapitalization.

Why This Rights Offering?

  • Balance Sheet Repair: The transaction reduced outstanding debt from substantial levels to just $120M with maturity extended to 2029, dramatically improving financial flexibility and reducing interest expense
  • Financial Distress Mitigation: The company was at a critical juncture where without intervention, the debt burden would have prevented any meaningful growth initiatives
  • Debt-to-Equity Conversion: APS (a major creditor) converted a substantial portion of its original debt investment into common equity ownership, de-leveraging the balance sheet
  • Sustainable Growth Path: The recapitalization created a healthier capital structure that would allow RTR to "grow in a more sustainable, healthy way and take advantage of the significant market for rental that continues to expand across the U.S."
  • Shareholder Protection & Participation: Despite the distressed situation, the rights offering gave existing shareholders the opportunity to participate at a discount and potentially avoid or reduce dilution from the debt conversion and new capital injection

Key Outcome & Strategic Significance:

The rights offering was fully backstopped by institutional investors (APS, STORY3, Nexus), meaning they guaranteed to purchase any unsubscribed shares. This backstop was essential—it ensured the restructuring wasn't dependent on shareholder participation, providing certainty of execution.

This was a rescue financing dressed as a growth recapitalization. The rights offering component, while relatively modest at $12.5M, was part of a larger strategic transaction designed to save the company and position it for long-term viability. The involvement of APS, STORY3, and Nexus signaled that sophisticated investors saw "tremendous upside potential" if the balance sheet issues could be resolved.

Lesson: Rights offerings in distressed situations often feature backstop agreements and are part of broader restructuring packages. They represent a lifeline for companies that need immediate capital and debt relief.

GCI Liberty (GLIBA)

"Growth Capital & Strategic Expansion"

Announcement Date: November 5, 2025
Offering Size: $300 million
Subscription Price: $27.20 per share (20% discount to 10-day VWAP)
Subscription Ratio: 0.3838 rights per share held (each whole right = 1 share)
Offering Period: November 26, 2025 - December 17, 2025
Final Result: Fully subscribed - 11,059,127 shares issued, ~95% via basic subscription

The Situation

GCI Liberty operates Alaska's largest telecommunications provider, serving more than 200 communities across a state larger than Texas, California, and Montana combined. Over 45 years, GCI has invested $4.7 billion in building and maintaining what is described as "Alaska's Most Advanced Network"—a hybrid fiber optic, satellite, and microwave system that connects some of the most remote communities in North America.

Unlike Rent the Runway, GCI Liberty came to market from a position of strength. The company wasn't facing financial distress; rather, it identified strategic opportunities that required substantial capital. The Board of Directors declared the rights offering as a dividend to shareholders, signaling confidence in the company's trajectory and giving shareholders first access to participate in the company's growth at an attractive price.

Why This Rights Offering?

  • Growth Capital Deployment: Fund working capital needs and substantial capital expenditures to continue expanding and modernizing Alaska's telecommunications infrastructure in underserved and remote areas
  • Infrastructure Investment: Support continued buildout of fiber networks, 5G deployment, and next-generation connectivity solutions. GCI operates critical infrastructure including the fiber optic cable system running undersea from Juneau to Seattle, connecting Alaska to the lower 48 states
  • Flexible Debt Management: Potential repayment or refinancing of outstanding indebtedness from a position of financial health, improving already-strong balance sheet metrics
  • Strategic M&A Capability: Pursue "highly accretive acquisitions in Alaska"—the company has identified potential targets that would strengthen market position and expand service offerings
  • Diversification Opportunities: Explore "opportunistic investments or partnerships" that could expand beyond Alaska telecommunications while leveraging existing expertise in remote connectivity
  • Shareholder-First Approach: Offering existing shareholders the ability to participate in growth at a 20% discount to market, maintaining or increasing their proportional ownership before any institutional capital was raised

Key Outcome & Strategic Significance:

The offering was remarkably successful, demonstrating exceptional shareholder confidence. It was fully subscribed with approximately 95% of shares taken via basic subscription privilege. This meant nearly all existing shareholders exercised their right to maintain their proportional ownership and participate in the company's growth story.

The oversubscription demand was intense—with only 5% of shares available for oversubscription allocation (roughly 552,956 shares), the subscription agent had to conduct multiple rounds of proration among oversubscribers. This competitive allocation process, detailed in the final prospectus, underscored the attractiveness of the investment opportunity.

Notably, no backstop was needed. The company raised nearly all $300 million from existing shareholders rather than requiring institutional guarantees. Major shareholder Dr. John Malone publicly announced his intention to fully exercise both basic and oversubscription privileges, sending a strong signal of confidence to the market.

Lesson: Rights offerings from financially healthy companies with clear growth strategies and strong shareholder bases can be executed without backstops and often see exceptional participation rates. The 95% basic subscription rate is considered a hallmark of a successful offering.

Internal Processing Mechanics

Critical SLA: The team has a 24-hour SLA to process and submit client responses to rights offerings. DTC's PSOP is open daily for rights submissions between 8:00 AM and 2:15 PM EST. These offers have hard market deadlines that cannot be extended. Missing the PSOP window means clients lose their opportunity to participate. Precision and speed are essential.

The 7-Step Internal Process

Here is the standardized workflow for processing rights offerings from entitlement through final settlement:

1
Deliver Free Trade of the Rights

For clients who have elected to participate in the offering, deliver the rights OFF their accounts via a free delivery (DVF) transaction. Rights are treated as separate securities with their own CUSIP and must be removed from client accounts to be submitted into the offer.

2
Receive vs. Payment (RVP) Trade for Basic Subscription

Process the basic subscription as a receive-versus-payment trade. Client pays the subscription price × number of shares subscribed. Receive common stock x subscription cost.

3
Segregate Transaction of the Entitlement

Execute a segregate transaction to move the common stock position from the client's general location to the restricted/segregated location

4
Cash Debit for Oversubscription Upon Submission

If client requests oversubscription, immediately debit their account for the full oversubscription cost (subscription price × oversubscription quantity requested) when submitting the rights to the offer via PSOP at DTC. This ensures the client has committed capital.

5
Reverse Debit When Oversubscription Finalized

After the issuer finalizes allocation and DTC distributes the allocation notice, reverse the original cash debit. The client may not receive their full oversubscription request due to proration.

6
Second RVP Trade for Final Oversubscription

Process a second RVP trade for the actual allocated oversubscription shares (which may be less than requested). Client pays subscription price × actual shares received.

7
Final Segregate When DTC Releases Common Stock

Execute final segregate transaction to move the newly issued common stock from segregation back to the client's trading account once DTC releases the shares and all settlements are complete.

Key Operational Concepts

PSOP

DTC's system for submitting and processing voluntary corporate actions, including rights offerings. Teams use PSOP to submit client instructions within the critical window. PSOP is open daily for rights submissions between 8:00 AM and 2:15 PM EST. Missing this window means the submission cannot be processed for that day.

Oversubscription Allocation

The issuer determines the final allocation method, which typically includes:

  • Pro-rata allocation: Based on the number of rights underlying the basic subscription (most common)
  • Multiple rounds of proration: As seen in GCI Liberty's offering, where no single proration factor existed
  • First-come-first-served: Rare, but sometimes used for smaller offerings

Why the Two-Stage Cash Debit?

The initial full debit (Step 4) locks in client commitment and ensures funds are available. The reversal and re-debit (Steps 5-6) accounts for the reality that clients rarely receive their full oversubscription request due to high demand and proration.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why would a financially healthy company like GCI Liberty do a rights offering instead of a traditional offering? +
Rights offerings offer several advantages even for healthy companies: (1) Lower costs - no underwriting fees, (2) Faster execution - less regulatory burden than traditional offerings, (3) Shareholder-friendly - gives existing shareholders first opportunity to participate at a discount before dilution, (4) Signal of confidence - when fully subscribed, demonstrates strong shareholder support, (5) Flexibility - can be used for specific strategic purposes like acquisitions or infrastructure investment.
What does it mean when a rights offering is "backstopped"? +
A backstop agreement means that one or more investors (typically institutional investors or existing major shareholders) commit to purchase any shares that are not subscribed for by other shareholders. This guarantees the company will raise the full amount intended. Rent the Runway's offering was fully backstopped by APS, STORY3, and Nexus, which was essential given the company's distressed financial situation. Backstops are more common in distressed situations or when the company needs certainty of full funding.
How is oversubscription typically allocated when there's high demand? +
The issuer determines the allocation methodology, which is disclosed in the prospectus. Most commonly, oversubscription shares are allocated pro-rata based on the number of rights underlying each participant's basic subscription privilege. This means larger shareholders get proportionally more of the oversubscription pool. In GCI Liberty's case, there were multiple rounds of proration among oversubscribers, which is typical when demand significantly exceeds available shares. The process continues until all available shares are allocated.
Why do we debit the full oversubscription amount upfront in Step 4? +
Debiting the full requested oversubscription amount serves multiple purposes: (1) Ensures the client has sufficient funds committed before submission to DTC, (2) Prevents the client from using those funds for other purposes during the allocation period, (3) Ensure BNY is not pre-funding, (4) Simplifies the subsequent reversal and re-debit process once actual allocation is known. This is standard industry practice for managing the uncertainty inherent in oversubscription requests.
What happens if a client doesn't exercise their rights? +
If a client doesn't exercise their rights, several outcomes are possible: (1) The rights expire worthless on the expiration date, (2) The client's ownership is diluted as other shareholders participate, (3) If the rights are tradeable, the client can sell them before expiration (in many offerings, the subscription agent will sell unexercised rights and distribute proceeds to shareholders), (4) The client's proportional ownership percentage decreases even though their absolute number of shares remains the same.
How does the subscription ratio affect dilution? +
The subscription ratio directly determines how many new shares will be issued relative to existing shares. A 1:1 ratio (1 right = 1 share) doubles the shares outstanding if fully subscribed, potentially halving ownership for non-participants. A 5:1 ratio (5 rights = 1 share) increases shares outstanding by 20% if fully subscribed. The ratio is set based on how much capital the company wants to raise and at what price. Lower ratios (fewer rights needed per share) create more potential dilution but raise more capital.
What's the significance of the 24-hour SLA and PSOP window? +
The 24-hour SLA is critical because: (1) Rights offerings have hard, non-negotiable market deadlines that cannot be extended, (2) PSOP (DTC's submission system) is only open between 8:00 AM and 2:15 PM EST daily for rights submissions - missing this window means waiting until the next day, (3) Late submissions mean clients lose their opportunity to participate, (4) Client decisions often come in at the last minute, requiring rapid processing, (5) Missing the deadline can result in significant financial harm to clients who intended to participate. This is why the team must be extremely efficient and organized in processing rights offering instructions.
Why did Rent the Runway need a rights offering if they were doing debt restructuring? +
The rights offering was part of a comprehensive recapitalization package. While APS was converting debt to equity, the rights offering gave existing shareholders the opportunity to participate and potentially avoid or reduce dilution from that conversion. It also raised additional capital ($12.5M) while being shareholder-friendly. The fact that it was fully backstopped meant the restructuring wasn't dependent on shareholder participation - the backstop investors would purchase any unsubscribed shares - but it gave shareholders the choice to maintain their ownership percentage at a discount.
What does "95% basic subscription" tell us about GCI Liberty's offering? +
This is an extremely positive signal indicating: (1) Strong shareholder confidence in the company and its growth strategy, (2) Shareholders wanted to maintain their pro-rata ownership, (3) The 20% discount was attractive relative to market price, (4) Only 5% of the offering was available for oversubscription, creating intense competition for those shares, (5) This led to multiple rounds of proration for oversubscribers, (6) The company successfully raised nearly all $300M from existing shareholders rather than needing external investors. This is considered a very successful rights offering outcome.

1. A client owns 10,000 shares of XYZ Corp. XYZ announces a rights offering with a subscription ratio of 5:1 (5 rights = 1 share). How many shares can the client purchase through their basic subscription privilege?
  • A) 10,000 shares
  • B) 2,000 shares
  • C) 5,000 shares
  • D) 50,000 shares
Correct Answer: B) 2,000 shares
Calculation: 10,000 shares owned ÷ 5 rights per share = 2,000 shares can be purchased
2. What is the PRIMARY reason Rent the Runway conducted their rights offering in August 2025?
  • A) To fund expansion into new markets
  • B) As part of a financial restructuring to reduce debt and improve their balance sheet
  • C) To acquire a competitor
  • D) To pay dividends to shareholders
Correct Answer: B
Rent the Runway was in financial distress and used the rights offering as part of a growth recapitalization to reduce debt from substantial levels to $120M and extend maturity to 2029.
3. In our internal process, why do we debit the client's account for the FULL oversubscription amount in Step 4, only to reverse it in Step 5?

  • D) Because DTC requires it
Correct Answer: B
We debit the full requested amount to ensure funds are committed and available, then reverse and re-debit for the actual allocated amount once the issuer finalizes proration.
4. A shareholder owns 2% of Company ABC before a rights offering. They decide NOT to participate in the offering. After the offering is completed and all other shareholders fully exercise their rights, what happens to this shareholder's ownership?
  • A) They still own 2% - nothing changes
  • B) Their ownership percentage decreases (dilution), though they still own the same number of shares
  • C) Their shares are automatically sold
  • D) They receive cash compensation for not participating
Correct Answer: B
When new shares are issued and the shareholder doesn't participate, their ownership is diluted. They own the same number of shares, but those shares represent a smaller percentage of the total outstanding shares.
5. What was the most significant difference between GCI Liberty's and Rent the Runway's rights offerings?
  • A) The subscription price discount
  • B) The subscription ratio
  • C) The strategic motivation - GCI was seeking growth capital from a position of strength, while RTR needed capital for financial restructuring
  • D) GCI's offering was tradeable and RTR's was not
Correct Answer: C
The key difference is motivation: GCI Liberty was a healthy company raising $300M for growth, infrastructure, and potential acquisitions. Rent the Runway was in financial distress and needed to restructure their balance sheet and reduce debt.
6. SCENARIO: You receive a client instruction at 4:30 PM to exercise both basic subscription and oversubscription for a rights offering that expires tomorrow. What is your team's immediate priority?

With a 24-hour SLA and PSOP only open between 8:00 AM and 2:15 PM EST, the team must act immediately to ensure the submission can be made during tomorrow's window. Late processing means the client loses their opportunity to participate, which could result in significant financial harm.
7. If GCI Liberty's rights offering had 95% basic subscription and the company issued 11,059,127 shares total, approximately how many shares were available for oversubscription allocation?

A backstop agreement means investors commit to purchase unsubscribed shares, guaranteeing the company raises the full intended amount. This was critical for Rent the Runway given their financial distress.

Summary & Key Takeaways

What You Should Remember

  • Rights offerings are a capital-raising tool that gives existing shareholders first opportunity to purchase new shares at a discount
  • The subscription ratio matters: It determines how many shares each shareholder can purchase (e.g., 5:1 means 5 rights needed for 1 share)
  • Same tool, different purposes: Companies use rights offerings for financial restructuring (Rent the Runway) or growth capital (GCI Liberty)
  • Dilution is real: Non-participating shareholders see their ownership percentage decrease, even though they own the same number of shares
  • Oversubscription is competitive: When demand exceeds supply (like GCI's 95% basic subscription), allocation is typically pro-rata
  • Operations require precision: 24-hour SLA, hard deadlines, seven-step process from entitlement to final settlement
  • Two-stage cash handling for oversubscription: Full debit on submission, reverse and re-debit based on actual allocation
